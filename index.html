The gap is not in resources. It is in method.

What Executive Simulations Actually Are

Executive simulations are immersive, scenario-based environments that replicate the pressures, ambiguity, and trade-offs of real leadership decisions: before the stakes are real. Unlike case studies (which are retrospective), simulations are prospective. You don't analyze what someone else did. The Global Leadership Forecast (DDI, 2023) found that 90% of leaders believe leadership development is critically important for organizational success: yet the programs most organizations run are not built for this environment.

Simulations are. They build the skills that matter most when conditions deteriorate: decision-making under pressure, emotional regulation, team coordination, and fast recovery from error.

Unlike an MBA: which takes years and costs tens of thousands of dollars: a well-designed executive simulation can be delivered in one to three days. And the learning it produces is encoded differently: not as theory recalled from memory, but as experience drawn from the body.

The Crisis Simulation: Building Decision-Making Under Pressure

"You don't rise to the occasion. You fall to the level of your training."

The Preparation Gap

Navy SEALs don't prepare for combat by reading about it. Airline pilots don't learn to handle engine failures by watching videos. Surgeons practice on simulators before their first real procedure. Why should executives be any different?

The data reveals a troubling gap: only 18% of managers receive formal training before assuming management responsibilities (Quarterdeck, 2025). Almost 60% of first-time managers receive no training during their transition at all. And yet the Corporate Executive Board estimates that 50%70% of new leaders fail within 18 months of taking on a role (CEB, cited in Blue Line Sims, 2025 and LSA Global, 2025).

These are not failures of character. They are failures of preparation.

What a Crisis Simulation Looks Like

A well-designed executive crisis simulation drops participants into a scenario with three defining features:

Time pressure: decisions must be made before all information is available

Ambiguity: the "right" answer isn't obvious, and advisors disagree

Consequences: choices cascade, and mistakes compound

A typical scenario: it is 6 AM on a Monday. A critical supplier has just shut down due to a political crisis. Your largest customer demands delivery by Friday. Your CFO says you cannot afford premium freight. Your PR team says the story is already leaking. Your board wants a call in two hours. What do you do first?

That question reveals how leaders prioritize under pressure, whether they communicate or retreat, whether they delegate or micromanage: behaviors that no job interview, 360-degree review, or MBA program reliably exposes.

Three Skills Simulations Build That Nothing Else Can

Cognitive stamina: the ability to think clearly when exhausted and overloaded with competing signals.

Decision velocity: the muscle memory of making good-enough decisions fast, rather than waiting for perfect information.

Emotional regulation: the capacity to project calm and confidence even when internally experiencing the storm everyone else is avoiding.

Research from SMG Group (2025) finds that effective leadership simulation training can produce a 25% increase in learning outcomes and a 20% improvement in overall job performance. Critically, these gains are most pronounced in exactly the high-stakes, time-pressured scenarios where untrained leaders most often fail.

Have you ever been thrown into a high-stakes situation without preparation? What do you wish you had practiced beforehand?

What War Games Taught Business Leaders About Strategy

"The Pentagon has used simulations for 150 years. Business is catching up."

The Prussian Insight

In the 1870s, the Prussian military developed a practice called Kriegsspiel: "war games": to train officers without risking lives. The concept was simple: simulate the battlefield, make decisions under uncertainty, and learn from failure in a controlled environment. Those exercises helped produce some of the most tactically sophisticated military forces the modern world had seen.

One hundred and fifty years later, the world's most sophisticated companies are borrowing the idea: and the data is validating it.

The Limits of Strategic Planning

There is a seductive lie at the heart of most strategic planning: the belief that because we have analyzed a situation thoroughly, we have prepared for it adequately. We have not.

Analysis tells us what could happen. Simulation teaches us how we will respond when it does.

Consider the difference: a strategic planning session might produce a 40-page document outlining three scenarios for a market downturn. But when that downturn actually hits: when the CFO is presenting the numbers, the board is restless, and operations is begging for budget: how will your leadership team actually behave? Will they fight over priorities, freeze, or execute with precision?

Research by McKinsey (cited in multiple leadership development analyses) consistently finds that 90% of new executives wish they had managed their leadership transition differently. Strategic knowledge, without the practiced capacity to deploy it under stress, is insufficient.

Three Wargaming Lessons That Transfer Directly to Business

Plans survive contact with the enemy only if your people trust each other. Military simulations reveal team dynamics fast: who steps up, who defers, who hoards information. Executive simulations surface the same dynamics.

The fog of war is the norm, not the exception. Leaders who have practiced making decisions with incomplete data are far less likely to be paralyzed by it in real situations.

Second and third-order consequences matter most. Experienced wargamers learn to ask not just "what will this decision do?" but "what will this decision cause?" Executive simulations build that exact habit.

Today, leading organizations including Shell (through its decades-long scenario planning tradition) and elite financial institutions have built internal simulation capabilities that they treat as core strategic infrastructure: not supplementary training. The wargaming insight has finally arrived in the boardroom.

Is your organization's strategy a living practice: or a document that gets revisited once a year? How do you keep your leadership team strategically sharp?

What they typically have not done is manage four simultaneous crises while their board demands answers, communicate with radical transparency while themselves uncertain, or make a $50 million decision in 90 minutes with 40% of the data they would normally want.

The Neuroscience Behind Why Simulations Work

"Your brain cannot tell the difference between a vivid simulation and reality. That is the entire point."

The Damasio Discovery

In 1994, neuroscientist Antonio Damasio published Descartes' Error: a landmark work that changed how we understand decision-making. His central finding: emotions are not the enemy of good decisions. They are essential to them.

Damasio's research on patients with prefrontal cortex damage revealed something counterintuitive: people who had lost the ability to feel emotions also lost the ability to make decisions: even when their analytical capabilities were completely intact. Without emotional signal, the brain could analyze endlessly but never choose.

This finding has profound implications for leadership development. Traditional methods are cognitively rich but emotionally thin. They produce knowledge. But knowledge does not automatically transfer to behavior: especially under stress.

How Simulation Encodes Differently

When a simulation is well-designed, it produces genuine emotional engagement: the anxiety of a ticking clock, the weight of a hard decision, the social pressure of being observed by peers. That emotional engagement encodes the experience more deeply in memory: more accessibly, more durably.

Research confirms this. Training Industry cites immersive simulations as producing learning that is retained because it is embedded in "memorable scenarios and actual practice" rather than passive presentation (Training Industry, 2024). The underlying mechanism is neurological: emotionally significant events are processed differently by the amygdala, producing stronger long-term potentiation in hippocampal memory pathways.

Stress Inoculation: A Military Concept With Executive Applications

Military researchers have studied what they call "stress inoculation": the process of exposing individuals to controlled stressors so that real stressors produce a more regulated response. The mechanism is neurological: repeated exposure to manageable stress modulates the amygdala's reactivity over time, producing calmer, more precise responses under actual high-stakes conditions.

This is precisely why the Canadian Armed Forces commissioned a study (published in the Journal of Leadership Education, 2025) on serious gaming simulation for leadership development. The findings supported the use of simulation for developing exactly the capabilities most degraded by real stress: emotional intelligence, strategic thinking, prioritization, and decisions under time pressure.

Applied to executive development: leaders who have practiced high-stakes decisions in simulations are neurologically calmer when facing real high-stakes decisions. Not because they are less stressed: but because their brains have filed a template for how to respond.

In a world where AI can deliver information instantly to any screen, the scarce resource is no longer knowledge. It is the trained capacity to act wisely under pressure. Simulations build that capacity at a neurological level.

Have you ever noticed the difference between reading about a skill and having actually practiced it under pressure? What was that gap like?

Why Executive Simulations Are More Important Now Than Ever

"We are not living through a crisis. We are living through an era of permanent volatility."

The Acceleration of Disruption

In the past five years, global business has absorbed a pandemic that shut down economies overnight, a land war in Europe, a generational inflation shock, the fastest interest rate hiking cycle in 40 years, a regional banking crisis, and the sudden emergence of AI as a force restructuring entire industries simultaneously.

The cruel irony of organizational life: organizations that most need to prepare for disruption are the least likely to do so. When things go well, investing in crisis preparation feels wasteful. When things are already bad, there is no time.

The window for preparation is always "before." But "before" feels abstract. The urgency is missing.

Executive simulations solve this by making "before" feel real. They import urgency into the preparation process. They make abstract scenarios concrete, emotional, and memorable. Recovering from wrong decisions fast: in fast-moving environments, waiting for certainty is itself a decision: often the wrong one. Executives who have practiced making fast decisions and course-correcting quickly are far more valuable.

The leaders who will define the next decade are not the ones with the most impressive résumés. They are the ones who have prepared: in the most realistic ways available: for a world that does not wait.

Looking at the past five years: what is the one thing you wish your organization had been better prepared for?

The Empathy Simulation: Building Emotionally Intelligent Leaders

"The hardest part of leadership is not the strategy. It is the humanity."

Daniel Goleman's research on emotional intelligence at the senior leadership level produced one of the most cited findings in organizational psychology: in a study comparing star performers to average ones in senior leadership positions, nearly 90% of the differences in their effectiveness could be attributed to emotional intelligence factors rather than cognitive abilities (Goleman, Harvard Business Review, 2004).

Technical skills and IQ matter at the executive level: but as Goleman put it, they are "threshold capabilities": entry-level requirements. The differentiator is EQ. And yet the vast majority of executive development programs spend the least time on it, and almost none on actually practicing it under pressure.

What Emotionally Demanding Simulations Look Like

The most powerful executive simulations include scenarios designed to be emotionally difficult: not just strategically complex:

Delivering devastating news to a team that trusts you, about a decision you did not make but must defend.

Navigating a conversation with a high-performer who is also creating a toxic environment.

Making a public statement about a company failure, with cameras on and facts still uncertain.

Choosing between two loyal colleagues for one open position, then living with the aftermath.

These scenarios are designed to activate emotional responses: and then require the leader to function at a high level despite them, not absent them. This distinction is critical. The goal is not emotional suppression. The research is clear that effective leaders do not attempt to suppress emotions; rather, they employ what researchers call "emotional agility": approaching emotions in a mindful and productive way (David, S., cited in PMC, 2020).

A separate Center for Creative Leadership study found that managers who show more empathy toward their direct reports are viewed as significantly better performers by their own supervisors: suggesting that empathy is not merely a "soft" skill but a measurable performance differentiator that flows upward in organizational assessments.

Simulations that generate genuine emotional engagement build EQ in ways that workshops about EQ cannot. The debrief that follows: helping participants connect their emotional responses to their leadership behavior and organizational impact: is where theoretical EQ becomes practiced EQ.

Why Experience Beats Theory Every Time, and What That Means for L&D

"Your organization's most expensive investment in leadership development may also be its least effective."

The $98 Billion Retention Problem

US corporations spent approximately $98 billion on corporate training in 2024: down slightly from $101.8 billion the previous year, but with a notable shift: spending on external content and services grew 23%, suggesting organizations are becoming more selective and investing in higher-quality interventions (Quarterdeck, 2025).

Despite this investment, 75% of leadership development professionals estimate that less than half of what they train actually gets applied on the job (Exec Learn, 2025). The forgetting curve: first documented by Hermann Ebbinghaus: shows that passive learning produces 5%20% retention after two weeks. We spend billions on methods that produce single-digit retention.

The problem is not the content. It is the delivery model.

The Learning Retention Gap

Edgar Dale's research on learning and retention (adapted into the widely cited "learning pyramid") provides a useful framework for understanding why passive methods underperform:

Lecture and reading: approximately 5%10% retention after two weeks

Audiovisual and demonstration: approximately 20%30% retention

Discussion and practice by doing: approximately 50%75% retention

Teaching others / immediate application: approximately 90% retention

Most executive development programs operate in the 5%30% tier. BusinessSimulations.com reports that simulation-based training achieves 75% better retention than traditional training: consistent with the "practice by doing" level of the retention model.

The Case Study Problem

Business schools have relied on case studies for over a century. The Harvard case method is brilliant at developing analytical thinking. But it has a structural limitation: it is backward-looking. You know the setting, characters, and constraints. You often know the outcome before you begin. And you are never truly in the situation: you are analyzing it from the outside, without the emotional weight of real consequences.

Executive simulations replace this distance with immersion. The scenario unfolds in real time. Information arrives incomplete and contradictory. You cannot pause the clock. You must decide now, with what you have.

The gap between analyzing a decision and making one is where the most valuable leadership learning lives. A 2023 HBS/KPMG study found that simulation-based leadership training produced measurable improvements in organizational performance metrics over a 29-month study period: results that no amount of case study analysis had previously produced (Training Industry, 2024).

Geopolitical Volatility and the Executive Who Prepared

"The executives who thrived through the past five years of global shocks had something in common: they had practiced for chaos."

The Supply Chain Lesson

In 2019, most global companies had supply chains optimized for efficiency. Just-in-time manufacturing. Single-source suppliers in low-cost markets. Lean inventory. It was elegant and profitable. It was also catastrophically brittle.

When COVID-19 struck in early 2020, those supply chains did not bend: they snapped. The executives scrambling to respond were doing something they had never practiced: making consequential decisions about geopolitical risk in real time, under pressure, without a playbook.

The cost of that unpreparedness is still being counted.

A New Kind of Executive Risk

We have entered a period of structural geopolitical volatility. Not a temporary disruption between stable eras, but a sustained condition of friction, realignment, and unpredictability across the global systems that business depends on.

The North American corporate leadership training market now places crisis management training and geopolitical resilience at the top of its investment priorities, driven: as Technavio's 2024 market analysis notes: by complex global supply chains and a recognition that leaders must be trained to respond to external shocks they cannot predict (Technavio, 2024).

Consider what executives must now navigate: trade policy that can shift in a single election cycle; critical mineral supplies controlled by geopolitically sensitive nations; cyber threats capable of disabling critical infrastructure in hours; regulatory environments increasingly used as instruments of geopolitical competition.

What Geopolitically-Focused Simulations Develop

The most sophisticated executive simulations now incorporate geopolitical scenarios designed for the current environment:

A sudden trade restriction on critical components with 48 hours' notice

A government nationalization threat affecting a key operational asset

A cyberattack on infrastructure launched by state actors

A rapid conflict escalation making a significant operational region untenable

These scenarios force executives to think like geopoliticians while behaving like business leaders: balancing financial obligations, employee welfare, stakeholder communication, and strategic repositioning simultaneously, under time pressure, in situations with no historical precedent.

The executives who emerged strongest from the disruptions of 2020%2024 were not the ones who had predicted those specific events. They were the ones who had built the leadership capability: through practice: to adapt to events they had not predicted. That capability is trainable.

Building Anti-Fragile Leaders for an Anti-Fragile World

"Resilience is no longer enough. The future belongs to leaders who get stronger under stress."

Beyond Resilience

Nassim Taleb introduced the concept of anti-fragility in his 2012 book Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder. Unlike resilient systems that absorb shocks and return to their previous state, anti-fragile systems improve because of volatility. They need disorder to grow stronger.

The concept was designed to describe complex systems. It maps perfectly onto the challenge of developing leaders for the world we now inhabit.

We have spent decades building resilient leaders: leaders who can withstand pressure and return to steady-state. That was sufficient when disruptions were episodic and recoveries were assured. The data suggests we are no longer in that world. The Russell Reynolds Global CEO Turnover Index (2025) found that the average tenure of outgoing CEOs globally dropped to 7.1 years in 2025, down from 8.3 years in 2021: as boards respond to a world where performance expectations are rising and external shocks are accelerating.

What Anti-Fragile Leadership Development Requires

Anti-fragile leadership is not built through information transfer. It is built through three mechanisms that simulations are uniquely positioned to provide:

Exposure to productive failure: anti-fragility is built through failure that produces learning rather than merely damage. Simulations create exactly this: a space where failure is safe, studied, and converted into future capability. Research from the MDPI Journal of Education (2025) confirms that simulation-based development uniquely bridges the gap between leadership preparation and actual performance by offering "practice-based tools" that cannot be replicated in classroom environments.

Variability over repetition: traditional training optimizes for the "right answer." Anti-fragile training optimizes for variability: exposing leaders to scenarios they have not seen, in combinations they did not expect. The muscle being built is not a specific response, but the capacity to generate responses.

Reflection as practice, not event: the structured debrief: honest, specific, connected to real patterns: is where transformation happens. The simulation is the stimulus. The reflection is the adaptation.

The Case for Investment Now

The leadership development market is projected to grow from $82.2 billion in 2024 to $193.2 billion by 2032: a compound annual growth rate of 11.29% (SNS Insider, 2025). That growth is being driven by a growing recognition that the old model: knowledge transfer in comfortable settings: is insufficient for the environment organizations now operate in.

Organizations that invest now in simulation-based executive development are building a compound advantage: leaders who are not just prepared for today's pressures, but who grow stronger each time they face new ones. That is the anti-fragile organization.

The leaders who will define the next decade are not the ones who waited for the crisis to prepare. They are the ones who manufactured the crisis first: safely, deliberately, and in service of becoming the leaders their organizations actually need.

What does the next generation of leadership development look like to you? I believe it is simulated, immersive, and emotionally honest. What is your vision?
